The 1 Arc Minute Rule

The single most important formula in LED

Your eye resolves angular detail down to roughly 1 arc minute — that's 1/60th of one degree. Above that threshold, two points blur into one. Below it, they remain distinct dots.

Every pixel pitch has a mathematical minimum viewing distance below which individual pixels become visible. Buying a panel finer than necessary is wasted money. Buying one coarser than necessary makes your brand look pixelated. The math is non-negotiable.

Minimum Viewing Distance (m) = Pixel Pitch (mm) × 3.438
3.438 = 1 ÷ tan(1 arc minute)

The three specs that fix 70% of problems

If you only check three numbers, check these

Most visual artifacts on a poorly-spec'd LED wall — flicker, banding, moiré on cameras, washed-out blacks — trace back to three numbers. Insist on these as minimums:

  • Refresh rate ≥ 3,840 Hz. Below this, every phone camera capturing your wall will show rolling black scan-bars. At 7,680 Hz the wall is camera-clean under any shutter speed.
  • Grayscale bit depth ≥ 14-bit. Below 14-bit, dark gradients (sunsets, skin tones, moody content) show visible stepping. The human eye's Weber contrast threshold is ~1% — 8-bit shadows step at 4%+ which is plainly visible. 14-bit steps are mathematically below your eye's detection limit.
  • Common Cathode architecture for fine pitch. Common Cathode uses separate optimised voltages for R/G/B chips, saving 31% power, running 20-25°C cooler, and delivering 67% longer LED life than Common Anode. Critical for 24/7 operation and broadcast-grade black levels.

The contrast trap

Why "1,000,000 : 1" is usually marketing

Every datasheet you'll see lists two contrast numbers. One is honest. One is theatre.

  • Static contrast measures black and white shown simultaneously (checkerboard pattern). This is what your eyes actually perceive in real content. Honest numbers fall between 3,000 : 1 and 25,000 : 1.
  • Dynamic contrast compares a full-white frame against a full-black frame, measured separately. Real content never produces this scenario. Dynamic numbers run into millions, but they don't reflect what you'll see on the wall.

When a vendor leads with "1,000,000 : 1 contrast" — they are quoting dynamic. Ask for the static contrast number. If they hesitate, walk.

The hidden cost: calibration

What gets cut from the cheap quote

LED panels drift. Without recalibration, your brand red becomes brand orange-red within 12-18 months. Every premium LED brand — Absen, Samsung, Sony, ourselves — calibrates in five layers:

  1. Pixel-level — individual R/G/B gain correction per LED.
  2. Module-level — module-to-module brightness uniformity.
  3. Cabinet-level — cabinet brightness & color matching.
  4. Wall-level — gamma, white balance (D65), color space.
  5. Content 3D LUT — creative grade, brand colour mapping.

Insist on a documented Delta E (ΔE) target. ΔE < 2 is broadcast-grade. ΔE < 4 is commercial-acceptable. Anything above 6 will be visibly inaccurate to a trained eye — and to your brand team.

7 questions to ask any LED vendor

  1. What is the static contrast (not dynamic)? Measured by what standard?
  2. What is the refresh rate, and is grayscale maintained at that refresh?
  3. What is the bit depth per colour channel?
  4. What is the calibration ΔE target? Done at factory or on-site?
  5. What is the driver IC and PSU brand? Tier-1 components or generic?
  6. Is post-install recalibration included? For how many years?
  7. What is the 5-year total cost — including power, maintenance, and parts?

If a vendor can't answer all seven without checking their phone, you are talking to a reseller, not an engineer. Find someone who can.
